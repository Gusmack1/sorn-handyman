Common Joinery Jobs in Scottish Terraced Homes

Rot repair, stuck doors, skirting runs — the jobs we see again and again on Ayrshire terraces.

Published 18 April 2026 · by Fraser Mackay

Scottish terraced houses — the sandstone rows in Kilmarnock, the miners' terraces in Cumnock, the ex-council streets in Mauchline and Auchinleck — share a handful of joinery problems that come up over and over. None are dramatic; all are easier to fix if you understand what's actually going on.

Here are the jobs we get called for most, what causes them, and roughly what they cost to put right.

1. Rot in external door frames and window cills

Scottish weather plus 100-year-old timber equals rot at the bottom of every external frame, eventually. It almost always starts at the joint between the frame and the cill, where water sits after rain and never fully dries.

The fix: if less than a third of the frame is soft, we cut out the rotten section with a saw and chisel, splice in a matching piece of hardwood or treated softwood, glue and fix it properly, then seal with wood primer and a microporous paint or stain. Typical cost £150–£280 per splice.

The full replacement call: if the whole cill has gone, or the rot extends up past the lock block, it's quicker and cheaper long-term to replace the whole frame. Budget £400–£700 for a standard single-leaf external frame, more for period doors with sidelights.

2. Doors that stick, drag or won't latch

This is the single most common joinery call we get. The door that's been fine for years suddenly won't close properly. Usually one of three things:

  • Hinges have worn. Pins rattle or the leaf has dropped. Often fixed by packing behind the hinge with a slip of cardboard or replacing the hinge entirely.
  • The frame has moved. Common in old tenements and terraces where the building has settled. The door itself is fine — the frame is out of square. Fix is usually to plane one edge and adjust the strike plate.
  • The door has swollen. Damp weather, especially in bathrooms and at back doors. Plane a couple of millimetres off the sticking edge, reseal the planed edge with primer so it doesn't swell again, and it'll last.

Typical cost: £65–£120 to take off, plane, rehang and adjust. Usually under an hour on site.

3. Skirting and architrave

Old Scottish terraces often have beautiful deep skirting (150–175mm) with torus or ogee profiles, and chunky architraves around every door. When kitchens or bathrooms get ripped out, folk often end up with mismatched modern skirting against original profile in the next room. It looks wrong instantly.

The fix: match the profile. Most timber merchants in Ayrshire stock three or four standard profiles, and for unusual ones we can spin a cutter on the router. A full room re-skirt (say a living room with four walls) is usually £180–£320 depending on profile complexity. Keep the originals where you can — they're solid pine, not MDF, and they'll outlast any replacement.

4. Loose or bouncy floorboards

Terraced floors are usually 6-inch pine boards nailed onto joists at 16-inch centres. After 80-plus years the nails work loose, joists dry out and shrink, and you get squeaks and bounce. The fix is straightforward but fiddly: lift individual boards carefully, screw them back down through existing holes into new ring-shank screws (not nails), and check for any damaged joists while you've got access. £120–£200 per room is typical.

5. Window sash repairs

Original sash-and-case windows are a bit of a specialism but absolutely worth saving if they're in reasonable nick. Common fixes:

  • Broken sash cords. Simple replacement — lift the staff bead, access the pocket, rethread new cord round the pulley. £55–£90 per sash.
  • Stuck sashes. Usually paint buildup in the channel. Score the paint, work the sash free, wax the channel. Half an hour.
  • Rattling sashes. Fit parting bead brushes or modern draught seals. Huge improvement on heat loss without changing the look.

6. Floor-to-ceiling built-ins in alcoves

Most terraced houses have chimney breasts with alcoves either side — perfect for built-in shelves or cupboards. Standard job: timber frame, MDF or birch ply carcasses, ply doors if you want them, and architrave to match existing. A pair of alcove cupboards with shelves above is usually 2 days on site plus materials — ballpark £650–£1,100 depending on finish.

7. Refitting tired doors rather than replacing

Original panelled internal doors are almost always better than anything you'd buy today. Don't bin them — refit them. Strip the paint, sand the faces, replace tired knobs and hinges, add new latches, and repaint in modern colours. You get a door with character that will outlast any B&Q replacement, usually for under £100 per door including paint.

Frequently asked questions

Can you repair rot without replacing the whole frame?

Often yes. If less than around a third of the frame is affected we cut out the rotten timber, splice in a matching hardwood or softwood piece, and reinstate the paint system. Full replacement only when the rot is structural.

Why do doors in old tenements always stick?

Three usual reasons — hinges worn or under-sized, the frame has moved with the building, or the door has swollen in damp weather. Usually an hour's work to plane, pack and rehang correctly.

What skirting matches a Victorian terrace?

Typically a torus or ogee profile around 145–170mm tall. We can source matching profiles from local merchants or cut a bespoke run on a router if the original is unusual.
