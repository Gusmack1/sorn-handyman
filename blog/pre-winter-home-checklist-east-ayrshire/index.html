Pre-Winter Home Checklist for East Ayrshire

Ten small jobs that save you a big callout bill come January.

Published 18 April 2026 · by Fraser Mackay

East Ayrshire doesn't get the worst Scottish winters — we're not in the Cairngorms — but we do get long wet spells, two or three hard frosts a year and the odd proper storm off the Atlantic. That combination finds every weakness in a house. A cracked tile nobody noticed in July becomes a ceiling stain in January. A blocked gutter turns into damp in the corner of a bedroom.

Here's the checklist we run for our regular customers in Sorn, Mauchline and Cumnock each autumn. None of it's dramatic. All of it is cheaper to do now than in an emergency.

1. Clear the gutters and downpipes

Top of the list for a reason. A blocked gutter backs up, overflows behind the fascia, and drips into the wall head. On an Ayrshire sandstone house that means months of water on the stone. On a modern harled house it means damp in an upstairs corner. Do it after most of the leaves are down — late October or November in East Ayrshire.

2. Book a boiler service

Get the boiler serviced before you need it. A proper service includes a combustion check, heat exchanger inspection and a gas-tightness test. Combi boilers that sit all summer often fire up in October with a stuck diverter valve or a tired pump. Far cheaper to catch it on a Tuesday in October than on a Saturday in January.

3. Lag the condensate pipe

The single most common boiler failure we see in a frost is a frozen condensate pipe — that white plastic pipe running out through the wall from the boiler. If it freezes, the boiler locks out and the house goes cold. A tenner of foam lagging and a dry-fit end cap solves it permanently.

4. Isolate and drain outside taps

Every outside tap should have an internal isolator. Turn it off, open the outside tap to drain it, leave the outside tap open. If there's no internal isolator, get one fitted — a 15-minute job that stops a burst pipe taking out a kitchen ceiling.

5. Check the loft insulation is in the right place

A surprising number of East Ayrshire lofts have the insulation shoved into the eaves, which blocks air flow and leaves the cold-water tank exposed. You want insulation across the whole loft floor, a ventilated gap at the eaves, and a lagged tank. While you're up there, check the loft hatch has draught strips — a huge amount of heat leaves through a bad hatch.

6. Draught-proof doors and letterboxes

Old external doors in Scottish homes are a classic heat leak. A new brush strip at the bottom, foam compression seal round the jamb and a brush inside the letterbox makes a noticeable difference. If the door itself is warped or rotted, that's a joinery job — get a quote before you spend money on strips that won't close the gap.

7. Service the extractor fans

Bathroom and kitchen extractor fans matter most in winter when windows stay closed. A fan clogged with dust runs hot and pulls hardly any air, which means condensation and mould in corners. 15 minutes to pop the cover, vacuum the blade and reset — or £30–£50 for a new fan if the motor's tired.

8. Walk the roof from the ground

You don't need to go up. Stand at the four corners of the house with binoculars or a phone zoom and look at every slope. You're hunting for slipped or cracked tiles, missing ridge mortar, lifted lead flashing, and any daylight showing through. Flag anything odd and get it looked at before the storms.

9. Check window seals and trickle vents

Run your hand round each window frame on a cold day. Feel a draught? The gasket's tired — often replaceable without a new window. Clean out the trickle vents at the top of each window too; they're almost always blocked with dust and the right balance of ventilation stops condensation forming overnight.

10. Put grit or salt somewhere handy

Last one, simple. A bag of rock salt by the back door saves a slip on the path at 7am in December. Councils in East Ayrshire grit the main roads but side streets and cul-de-sacs in Cumnock, Mauchline and Sorn often don't get touched.

Frequently asked questions

When should I do these winter checks?

Aim for late September or early October in East Ayrshire. Gutters ideally get done after most of the leaves are down, and you want to catch boiler and pipework issues before the first proper frost hits in November.

What's the single most important winter job on a Scottish house?

Lag external pipework — the overflow from a cold-water tank, any condensate pipe from a combi boiler, and outside taps. A burst condensate on a frosty morning in Cumnock will shut the boiler off and the callout queue is long.

Can you do the whole checklist in one visit?

Usually yes — a typical pre-winter half-day visit covers gutters, draught-proofing, pipe lagging, a boiler pressure check and any outside tap isolation. Roughly £150–£220 depending on the size of the house.
